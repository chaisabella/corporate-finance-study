Topic 1 · Capital Structure & Valuation

8/9 EXAMS · 20–25 PTS
Pattern Industry portfolio → unlever to find $r_U$ → re-lever to your firm → compute WACC → value $V_L$ → compare to APV.
Topic 1 Tutorial

The big question this topic answers

You're considering investing in a project. To know if it's worth it, you need to compare the project's expected returns against what investors require. That required return is called the cost of capital — it's the toll the project has to pay for using other people's money. If the project earns more than the cost of capital, it adds value. If it earns less, it destroys value.

Why a company has two costs of capital, not one

Companies fund themselves with two kinds of money: debt (loans, bonds — fixed payments to lenders) and equity (shares — leftover profit goes to shareholders). These two have different costs:

  • Debt is cheaper. Lenders get paid first, so their risk is low → they accept a lower return ($r_D$). Plus, interest is tax-deductible — the IRS effectively subsidizes part of it.
  • Equity is more expensive. Shareholders get whatever's left after debt is paid → their risk is higher → they demand a higher return ($r_E$).

The blend of these two — weighted by how much debt vs equity the company uses — is called WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital).

A roommate situation. You and your roommate split rent. Your share is "equity" (you're the residual claimant — you get what's left of the apartment's value after costs). The bank holding the building's mortgage is "debt" (gets paid every month, no surprises, no upside). The total monthly cost of housing the apartment is the WACC of the building.

The trick this topic uses: "unlevering"

Here's the problem. You want to invest in, say, a retail clothing project. But your current company isn't in retail clothing. So you can't just look at your own equity returns and call it good — your existing business has different risk.

Instead, you look at other firms already in retail clothing. Those firms have their own debt levels, which inflates their equity risk. To get the pure business risk of retail clothing, you have to mathematically strip out the debt effect. That's unlevering. What's left is $r_U$ — the return you'd require if the project were funded entirely with equity. It's a property of the business, not the financing.

Then you can re-lever back up using your own firm's debt mix to get the equity cost you'd actually face.

Two valuation methods (and why they sometimes disagree)

Once you know $r_U$, there are two ways to value a levered project:

  • WACC method — bake the tax savings from debt into the discount rate. Use this when the firm maintains a constant debt-to-value ratio (debt grows as the firm grows).
  • APV method — value the firm as if all-equity, then add the present value of the tax savings separately. Use this when debt is a fixed dollar amount that doesn't change.

They give slightly different answers because they make different assumptions about how risky the tax savings are. WACC says the tax shield is as risky as the firm itself (discount at $r_U$). APV says the tax shield is as risky as the debt contract (discount at $r_D$, which is lower). Lower discount rate → higher PV → APV usually gives a slightly higher value.

What you'll be asked

Pretty much every Bodnar exam: given an industry portfolio's data, find $r_U$. Then given your firm's debt structure, find WACC, value the project, and confirm with APV or Flow-to-Equity. The numbers change; the structure doesn't.

TYPE 1A

Unlevering Beta → finding $r_U$

Appears in: S19 · S20 · S22 · S23 · F22 · F23 · F24 · F25 (9/9)
What's actually happening here

You're looking at other firms in the same business and asking: "If I stripped away their loans, what would their pure business risk look like?" That stripped-down risk is what your project would face too — because you're going into the same line of work.

Beta ($\beta$) is just a number that measures how risky a stock is compared to the market. $\beta = 1$ means the stock moves exactly with the market. $\beta = 1.5$ means it swings 50% more than the market. The higher the beta, the higher the required return.

Each firm has an equity beta (the risk of its shares, which includes leverage) and a debt beta (usually small, since debt is safer). Combine them, weighted by how much of each the firm uses, and you get the asset beta ($\beta_U$) — the underlying business risk.

The two equivalent methods

Method 1 · Beta-based (preferred)
$$\beta_U = \frac{D}{V}\cdot\beta_D + \frac{E}{V}\cdot\beta_E$$
$$r_U = r_F + \beta_U \cdot E(\text{MRP})$$
Take the weighted average of debt risk and equity risk to get business risk. Then plug into CAPM to convert that risk into a required return.
Method 2 · Returns-based (also fine)
$$r_E = r_F + \beta_E \cdot E(\text{MRP})$$
$$r_U = \frac{D}{V}\cdot r_D + \frac{E}{V}\cdot r_E$$
Use CAPM to find the equity return first, then weight it against the debt return.
The trap Use the industry portfolio's $D/V$, $\beta_E$, $\beta_D$, $r_D$ to find $r_U$ — not your firm's. The whole point is to learn the asset risk from people already doing this kind of work. Once you have $r_U$, you switch to your firm's $D/V$ for the next steps.
★ Worked Example F25 Q1b · 4 pts
Q. Retail clothing industry portfolio: $\beta_E = 1.65$, $\beta_D = 0.313$, $D/V = 0.45$, $r_D = 6.5\%$. Market: $r_F = 4\%$, MRP $= 8\%$. Find $r_U$ for retail clothing.

Step 1. Use industry data only. Apply the beta formula:

β_U = 0.45 · 0.313 + 0.55 · 1.65
    = 0.141 + 0.908
    = 1.05

Step 2. Plug into CAPM:

r_U = 4% + 1.05 · 8%
     = 4% + 8.4%
     = 12.4%
$r_U = 12.4\%$
DRILL 1A.1 · Industry $\beta_E = 1.45$, $\beta_D = 0.25$, $D/V = 0.30$, $r_D = 5.5\%$. Market: $r_F = 4\%$, MRP $= 6\%$. Find $r_U$.
▸ Show solution
$\beta_U = 0.30 \cdot 0.25 + 0.70 \cdot 1.45 = 0.075 + 1.015 = $ 1.09
$r_U = 4\% + 1.09 \cdot 6\% = $ 10.54%

Source: S19 Q1a / S23 Q1a

DRILL 1A.2 · Industry $\beta_E = 1.65$, $D/V = 0.30$, $r_D = 5.35\%$ ($\beta_D = 0.15$). Market: $r_F = 4.6\%$, MRP $= 5\%$. Find $r_U$ using both methods.
▸ Show solution
Beta way: $\beta_U = 0.30 \cdot 0.15 + 0.70 \cdot 1.65 = 0.045 + 1.155 = $ 1.20 → $r_U = 4.6\% + 1.2 \cdot 5\% = $ 10.6%

Returns way: $r_E = 4.6\% + 1.65 \cdot 5\% = 12.85\%$; $r_U = 0.30 \cdot 5.35\% + 0.70 \cdot 12.85\% = 1.605\% + 8.995\% = $ 10.6%

Source: F24 Q3b

TYPE 1B

The WACC Method · valuing $V_L$ when $D/V$ is constant

Appears in: S19 · S20 · S22 · S23 · F22 · F23 · F24 · F25 (9/9)
What's actually happening here

You found $r_U$ — the cost of capital if the firm were all-equity. Now you adjust it downward to reflect the tax savings from using debt. That's WACC.

Why is WACC lower than $r_U$? Because interest is tax-deductible. Every dollar the firm pays in interest reduces its taxable income by a dollar, so the effective cost of debt isn't $r_D$ — it's $r_D \cdot (1 - \tau_C)$. The bigger your debt ratio, the bigger this discount.

WACC is what you discount the unlevered free cash flows by when the firm maintains a constant debt ratio. If the firm is going to keep $D/V$ at, say, 20% as it grows, debt grows with it, tax shield grows with it, and WACC bakes all that in.

The formulas

The Easy Formula (use this)
$$r_{\text{WACC}} = r_U - \frac{D}{V}\cdot r_D \cdot \tau_C$$
Start with the unlevered cost of capital, then subtract the tax savings from debt.
The Long Formula (also valid)
$$r_{\text{WACC}} = \frac{E}{V}\cdot r_E + \frac{D}{V}\cdot r_D \cdot (1 - \tau_C)$$
where $\;\;r_E = r_U + \dfrac{D}{E}(r_U - r_D)$
Weighted average of after-tax debt cost and equity cost. The $r_E$ formula re-levers the unlevered cost back up to your firm's actual debt mix.
Then use WACC to value the project
$$V_L = \frac{\text{FCF}_1}{r_{\text{WACC}} - g}\;\;\text{(growing perpetuity)}$$
$$V_L = \frac{\text{FCF}}{r_{\text{WACC}}}\;\;\text{(flat perpetuity, } g=0\text{)}$$
$$E = V_L \cdot \frac{E}{V},\quad D = V_L \cdot \frac{D}{V}$$
Discount the cash flows at WACC to get the levered firm value. Then split it into equity and debt portions using your target ratios.
★ Worked Example F25 Q5c · 4 pts
Q. $r_U = 11.2\%$, $D/V = 0.20$, $r_D = 4.90\%$, $\tau_C = 25\%$. FCF $= \$12.32$M perpetuity. Find $E$ using WACC.
r_WACC = 11.2% − (0.20)(4.90%)(25%)
         = 11.2% − 0.245%
         = 10.955%

V_L = $12.32M / 0.10955 = $112.46M
E = V_L · (E/V) = $112.46 · 0.80 = $89.97M
D = V_L · (D/V) = $112.46 · 0.20 = $22.49M
$E = \$89.97$M

Re-levering: from $r_U$ back up to $r_E$

If they ask you to find your firm's $r_E$ (different from the industry's), you re-lever using your $D/V$:

$$\beta'_E = \beta_U + \frac{D}{E}(\beta_U - \beta_D)$$
$$r_E = r_U + \frac{D}{E}(r_U - r_D)$$
More debt means more risk for equity holders, so equity beta and equity return both go up.
DRILL 1B.1 · $r_U = 8.0\%$, $D/V = 0.4$, $r_D = 4.0\%$, $\tau_C = 35\%$. Find $r_{\text{WACC}}$. Then with FCF $= \$50$M perpetuity, find $V_L$.
▸ Show solution
$r_{\text{WACC}} = 8.0\% - 0.4 \cdot 4.0\% \cdot 0.35 = 8.0\% - 0.56\% = $ 7.44%
$V_L = \$50\text{M} / 0.0744 = $ \$672.04M

Source: S22 Q1c

DRILL 1B.2 · $r_U = 10.6\%$, $D/V = 0.20$, $r_D = 5.10\%$, $\tau_C = 30\%$, $g = 4\%$, $\text{FCF}_1 = \$34.4$M. Find $V_L$.
▸ Show solution
$r_{\text{WACC}} = 10.6\% - 0.20 \cdot 5.10\% \cdot 0.30 = 10.6\% - 0.306\% = $ 10.294%
$V_L = \$34.4\text{M} / (0.10294 - 0.04) = \$34.4\text{M} / 0.06294 = $ \$546.55M

Source: F24 Q3d/e

TYPE 1C

The APV Method · valuing $V_L$ when debt is fixed

Appears in: S19 · S20 · S22 · S23 · F22 · F23 · F25 (7/9)
When to use APV vs WACC

WACC assumes debt scales with firm value. If the project becomes more valuable, you take on more debt to keep $D/V$ constant. The tax shield therefore depends on the firm's value — making it as risky as the firm itself.

APV assumes a fixed dollar level of debt. You borrow $\$22.5$M and that's it — even if the firm grows, the debt stays the same. The tax shield is now tied to a fixed contract, not the firm's value, so it's less risky.

Both methods value the same firm, just under different assumptions about how the tax shield behaves. APV usually gives a slightly higher answer because it treats the tax shield as less risky → discount at $r_D$ (lower) → higher present value.

APV with PERMANENT debt (the easy case)
$$V_L = V_U + D \cdot \tau_C$$
$$E = V_L - D$$
When debt is permanent, the tax shield is a perpetuity of $D \cdot r_D \cdot \tau_C$ discounted at $r_D$, which simplifies beautifully to just $D \cdot \tau_C$.
APV with non-permanent fixed debt
$$\text{ITS}_t = D \cdot r_D \cdot \tau_C \;\;\;\text{(each period)}$$
$$\text{PV(ITS)} = \sum_{t} \frac{\text{ITS}_t}{(1 + r_D)^t}$$
$$V_L = V_U + \text{PV(ITS)}$$
Compute the tax shield each year, discount at the debt rate (because the shield is contractual, not market-driven), add to the unlevered value.
★ Worked Example F25 Q5e · 4 pts
Q. $V_U = \$110$M, $D = \$22.5$M (permanent), $r_D = 4.90\%$, $\tau_C = 25\%$. Find $E$ using APV.
ITS each year = $22.5M · 4.90% · 25% = $0.2756M
PV(ITS) = $0.2756 / 0.049 = $5.625M
    // shortcut: D · τ_C = $22.5 · 0.25 = $5.625M ✓

V_L = $110M + $5.625M = $115.625M
E = V_L − D = $115.625 − $22.5 = $93.125M
$E = \$93.125$M (vs WACC's $\$89.97$M)
The "Why is APV different from WACC?" question Bodnar loves this one. The answer: under APV with permanent debt, the tax shield is treated as a contractual perpetuity discounted at $r_D$ (low risk → high PV). Under WACC, the tax shield scales with firm value, so it's discounted at $r_U$ (higher risk → lower PV). Same dollar amount of tax savings, two different risk assumptions, two different values.
DRILL 1C.1 · $V_U = \$625$M. Permanent $D = \$168$M, $r_D = 3.5\%$, $\tau_C = 35\%$. Find $V_L$ via APV.
▸ Show solution
$V_L = \$625\text{M} + \$168\text{M} \cdot 0.35 = \$625\text{M} + \$58.8\text{M} = $ \$683.8M

Source: S22 Q1f

TYPE 1D

Flow-to-Equity (FTE) · confirming $E$ directly

Appears in: S19 · S20 · S23 · F23 · F24 · F25 (6/9)
What's actually happening here

WACC values the whole firm and then splits off equity. FTE values just the equity directly. You compute the cash that flows to shareholders after debt holders take their cut, then discount at the equity cost of capital.

The two methods always give the same answer. Bodnar uses FTE as a "verify your work" question, almost always right after WACC.

Step 1 · FCFE (cash that flows to equity)
$$\text{FCFE} = \text{FCF} - r_D \cdot D \cdot (1 - \tau_C) + \Delta D$$
Start with the unlevered free cash flow, subtract after-tax interest, add any new debt issuance. When debt is constant, $\Delta D = 0$.
Step 2 · Discount at $r_E$
$$r_E = r_U + \frac{D}{E}(r_U - r_D)$$
$$E = \frac{\text{FCFE}}{r_E}\;\;\text{(perpetuity)}$$
★ Worked Example F25 Q5d · 4 pts
Q. FCF $= \$12.32$M, $D = \$22.49$M (from WACC step), $r_D = 4.90\%$, $\tau_C = 25\%$, $r_U = 11.2\%$, $D/E = 0.25$. Confirm $E$ using FTE.
r_E = 11.2% + 0.25 · (11.2% − 4.90%)
      = 11.2% + 1.575%
      = 12.775%

After-tax interest = $22.49M · 4.90% · 0.75 = $0.8265M
FCFE = $12.32M − $0.8265M = $11.4935M

E = $11.4935M / 0.12775 = $89.97M ✓ (matches WACC)
$E = \$89.97$M (confirms WACC)

Topic 2 · Project Evaluation & FCF

5/9 EXAMS · 25–30 PTS
Pattern Multi-part question: build FCF → find $r_U$ → compute $V_U$ → switch to WACC for $V_L$ → check with debt + ITS → sometimes a leverage-change comparison.
Topic 2 Tutorial

What "free cash flow" actually means

Forget what accounting income looks like — that number is full of fictions like depreciation that don't represent real cash. Free Cash Flow (FCF) is the actual cash the project generates after everyone gets paid: suppliers, employees, the IRS, and anyone the project owes for new equipment or inventory.

Why it's called "free" — because it's the cash that's available to be paid out to investors (debt holders + equity holders) without hurting the business. It's the only number that matters for valuation.

Your monthly take-home. You earn a salary (revenue). Rent, groceries, and utilities go out (operating costs). Taxes get withheld (corporate tax). You buy a new laptop you needed (capital expenditure). You restock your skincare (working capital). What's left at the end of the month is what you can save or send home — that's your free cash flow.

The depreciation trick

Depreciation is the most confusing part of the formula because it shows up twice — once with a minus, once with a plus. Here's why:

Depreciation is a non-cash expense. The company bought a machine 3 years ago for $\$60$M. Accounting rules let it pretend the machine is "wearing out" by, say, $\$10$M per year. That $\$10$M shows up on the income statement as an expense — even though no actual cash leaves the bank account this year.

Why does the firm bother? Because that $\$10$M expense lowers taxable income, which lowers the tax bill. So depreciation = a tax shield on a phantom expense.

In the FCF formula, you subtract depreciation inside the parentheses (so the tax calculation is correct) and then add it back outside (because no actual cash left the business). The net effect: you save tax money on a fake expense. Genius.

Working capital and capex

Two more cash flows that aren't in the income statement:

  • $\Delta$NWC — when a business grows, it needs more inventory, more receivables, more cash on hand. That's real cash tied up that you don't get to pay out. So it gets subtracted from FCF.
  • CapEx — buying new long-term assets (factories, machines, software). Real cash leaving today, even though accounting will spread the expense over years via depreciation.

Time zero is special

At $t = 0$, the project hasn't done anything yet. There are no revenues, no operating costs, no taxes. There's just the upfront investment: buying the equipment and stocking up on initial working capital. So $\text{FCF}_0$ is purely negative — the cash you spend to get the project started.

TYPE 2A

Computing Unlevered Free Cash Flow

Appears in: S19 · S20 · S23 · F22 · F24 + parts of others (7/9)
The One Formula That Matters
$$\text{FCF} = (\text{Rev} - \text{costs} - \text{dep})(1 - \tau_C) + \text{dep} - \Delta\text{NWC} - \text{CapEx} + \text{ATPDisp}$$
Operating profit after tax (with dep added back), minus growth investments (working capital + new equipment), plus any after-tax proceeds from selling old equipment.
★ Worked Example F24 Q3a · 5 pts
Q. Rev $=\$400$M, COGS $=\$200$M, SG&A $=\$120$M, Dep $=\$20$M, $\Delta\text{NWC} = \$1.6$M, CapEx $=\$26$M, $\tau_C = 30\%$, ATPDisp $=\$0$. Find $\text{FCF}_1$.
Cash costs = COGS + SG&A = $200M + $120M = $320M

FCF = ($400 − $320 − $20) · (1 − 0.30) + $20 − $1.6 − $26 + 0
      = $60 · 0.70 + $20 − $1.6 − $26
      = $42 + $20 − $1.6 − $26
      = $34.4M
$\text{FCF}_1 = \$34.4$M

Time Zero is Special

At $t = 0$, the project hasn't generated anything yet. So $\text{FCF}_0 = -\text{initial CapEx} - \text{initial NWC}$. All negative.

DRILL 2A.1 · Initial CapEx $=\$50$M, Initial NWC $=\$5$M. Find $\text{FCF}_0$.
▸ Show solution
$\text{FCF}_0 = -\$50\text{M} - \$5\text{M} = $ −\$55M
DRILL 2A.2 · Rev $=\$22.5$M, costs $=\$15$M, dep $=\$2.5$M, $\Delta\text{NWC} = 0$, CapEx $=\$2.5$M (= dep), $\tau_C = 30\%$. Find $\text{FCF}_1$.
▸ Show solution
$\text{FCF} = (\$22.5 - \$15 - \$2.5) \cdot 0.70 + \$2.5 - 0 - \$2.5 = \$5 \cdot 0.70 + 0 = $ \$3.5M

Source: S19 Q1b

DRILL 2A.3 · Rev $=\$30$M, costs $=\$15$M, dep $=\$2.5$M, $\Delta\text{NWC} = 0$, CapEx $=\$2.5$M, $\tau_C = 20\%$. Find $\text{FCF}_1$.
▸ Show solution
$\text{FCF} = (\$30 - \$15 - \$2.5) \cdot 0.80 + \$2.5 - 0 - \$2.5 = \$12.5 \cdot 0.80 = $ \$10M

Source: S23 Q1b

TYPE 2B

After-Tax Salvage Cash Flow

Appears in: S20 · F22 · F23 · F24 · F25 (5/9)
When this comes up

If you're selling old equipment at the end of the project, the cash you get isn't just the sale price. There's a tax adjustment. If you sell for more than the asset's accounting "book value," that gain is taxable. So you owe the IRS some of the proceeds.

$$\text{ATPDisp} = \text{Salvage} - (\text{Salvage} - \text{Book Value}) \cdot \tau_C$$
Sale price minus the tax owed on the gain. If salvage equals book value, no tax. If asset is fully depreciated (book = 0), tax is owed on the entire salvage.
DRILL 2B.1 · Sell asset for $\$40$M, book value $=\$10$M, $\tau_C = 30\%$. Find after-tax salvage.
▸ Show solution
ATPDisp $= \$40\text{M} - (\$40\text{M} - \$10\text{M}) \cdot 0.30 = \$40\text{M} - \$9\text{M} = $ \$31M

Subtract the initial investment from the present value of future cash flows.

Q. Investment $=\$100$M today. FCF $=\$12.32$M perpetuity (no growth). $r_U = 11.2\%$. Find $V_U$ and NPV.
V_U = $12.32M / 0.112 = $110M

Topic 3 · Options

9/9 EXAMS · 15–25 PTS
Pattern Almost always 2 sub-questions: price the option (replicating portfolio AND/OR risk-neutral AND/OR Black-Scholes), then verify with put-call parity. Often paired with a comparative-statics question.
Topic 3 Tutorial

What an option actually is

An option is a contract that gives you the right, but not the obligation, to do something at a future date at a price you lock in today.

Two flavors:

  • Call option: the right to buy something at a fixed price (the strike). You buy a call if you think the price will go up.
  • Put option: the right to sell something at a fixed price. You buy a put if you think the price will go down — or as insurance.
A call is like a non-refundable reservation deposit. You put $\$50$ down today to lock in a flight at $\$300$ for next year. If the flight ends up costing $\$500$, you exercise (use the deposit, pay the $\$300$, save $\$200$). If it ends up costing $\$200$, you walk away — you lose the $\$50$ deposit but at least you're not stuck overpaying. The deposit itself is the option premium. The $\$300$ locked-in price is the strike.

The big puzzle: how do you price an option?

Pricing an option seems impossible because it depends on what the stock does in the future, which nobody knows. But finance found three clever ways to do it without needing to predict the future.

Method 1 — Replicating Portfolio (binomial). Build a portfolio of stock + risk-free bond that has exactly the same payoffs as the option in every possible future scenario. By a no-arbitrage argument, two things with identical future cash flows must have the same price today. So the option price = the cost of building that portfolio. No prediction needed.

Method 2 — Risk-Neutral Probability (binomial). Pretend everyone in the market is indifferent to risk. Find the "fake" probability that makes the stock's expected return equal the risk-free rate. Then the option price = expected option payoff under that fake probability, discounted at the risk-free rate. Different math, same answer as Method 1.

Method 3 — Black-Scholes. When the future has more than 2 outcomes (continuous price movements with normal-distributed returns), use this famous formula. It looks scary but follows the same logic: probability-weighted payoff minus probability-weighted cost.

Volatility is the secret sauce

The single most important thing about an option is the volatility ($\sigma$) of the underlying. Higher volatility = bigger possible moves = bigger possible payoffs. Both calls and puts get more valuable when volatility goes up, because the downside is capped (you just don't exercise) but the upside is uncapped.

If you ever see a comparative-statics question — "what if volatility went up?" — the answer is almost always: option prices rise.

Put-Call Parity (the free points)

This is one equation that links call prices to put prices. If you know any 3 of the 4 inputs, you can solve for the 4th:

$$S + P = C + \text{PV}(K)$$

Stock $+$ Put $=$ Call $+$ safe bond paying $K$. Both sides have the same payoff at maturity ($\max(S, K)$), so they must cost the same today.

TYPE 3A

Replicating Portfolio Method (Binomial)

8/9
What's actually happening here

You're building a "fake option" out of stock and risk-free bonds. If you buy $N$ shares and lend $M$ dollars in bonds, the portfolio has a payoff in each future scenario. Pick $N$ and $M$ such that the portfolio payoff matches the option payoff in both up and down states. The cost to build that portfolio today = the option's price today.

Replicating Portfolio Recipe
1
Compute option payoffs. Call: $\max(P - K, 0)$. Put: $\max(K - P, 0)$. In both up and down states.
2
Set up two equations. $N \cdot P_{\text{up}} + M \cdot K = $ Payoff$_{\text{up}}$ ; $N \cdot P_{\text{down}} + M \cdot K = $ Payoff$_{\text{down}}$.
3
Solve for $N$ first (delta method): $N = \dfrac{C_{\text{up}} - C_{\text{down}}}{P_{\text{up}} - P_{\text{down}}}$. Then back-solve for $M$.
4
Price = portfolio cost today. Option $= N \cdot P_0 + M \cdot \text{PV}(K)$ where PV$(K) = K / (1 + r_F)$.
★ Worked Example F25 Q2a · 4 pts
Q. Stock $=\$50$ today, will be $\$75$ (up) or $\$30$ (down) in 1 year. $K = \$52$. $r_F = 4\%$. Find call price using replicating portfolio.

1. Payoffs: Up $= \max(75 - 52, 0) = \$23$; Down $= \max(30 - 52, 0) = \$0$

2. Equations:

75N + 52M = 23
30N + 52M = 0

3. Subtract: $45N = 23$ → $N = 23/45 = $ 0.5111.
Back-solve: $30(0.5111) + 52M = 0$ → $M = -15.333/52 = $ −0.2949 (negative = borrowing)

4. Price today:

Call = 0.5111 · $50 + (−0.2949) · ($52/1.04)
      = $25.555 − $14.745
      = $10.81
Call $= \$10.81$
DRILL 3A.1 · Asset $\$50$M now, $\$100$M up or $\$25$M down in 1 yr. $K = \$60$M. $r_F = 5\%$. Find call price.

TYPE 3B

Risk-Neutral Probability Method

8/9
What's actually happening here

The trick: pretend everyone is indifferent to risk. In that imaginary world, every asset would earn the risk-free rate. Find the probability that makes the stock's expected return equal $r_F$ — that's the "risk-neutral probability." It's not the real probability, just a mathematical construct.

Then the option price is just the expected payoff (under that fake probability) discounted at $r_F$. Same answer as the replicating portfolio, just easier algebra in some cases.

Risk-Neutral Recipe
1
Compute up/down returns: $R_{\text{up}} = P_{\text{up}}/P_0 - 1$ ; $R_{\text{down}} = P_{\text{down}}/P_0 - 1$.
2
Solve for ProbRN(up): $\text{ProbRN} \cdot R_{\text{up}} + (1 - \text{ProbRN}) \cdot R_{\text{down}} = r_F$.
3
Compute expected option payoff: $E(\text{payoff}) = \text{ProbRN} \cdot \text{Payoff}_{\text{up}} + (1 - \text{ProbRN}) \cdot \text{Payoff}_{\text{down}}$.
4
Discount at $r_F$: Option price $= E(\text{payoff}) / (1 + r_F)$.
★ Worked Example F25 Q2b · 4 pts
Q. Stock $\$50$ → $\$75$ or $\$30$. $K = \$52$. $r_F = 4\%$. Price the put via risk-neutral.

1. Returns: $R_{\text{up}} = 75/50 - 1 = 50\%$ ; $R_{\text{down}} = 30/50 - 1 = -40\%$

2. Solve probability:

ProbRN · 50% + (1 − ProbRN) · (−40%) = 4%
50%·p − 40% + 40%·p = 4%
90%·p = 44%
ProbRN = 0.4889

3. Put payoffs: Up $= \max(52-75, 0) = \$0$ ; Down $= \max(52-30, 0) = \$22$

E(payoff) = 0.4889 · $0 + 0.5111 · $22 = $11.244

4. Discount:

Put = $11.244 / 1.04 = $10.81
Put $= \$10.81$
TYPE 3C

Black-Scholes Option Pricing

Appears in: S19 · S22 · S23 · F22 · F23 (5/9)
What's actually happening here

When the future isn't binomial — when prices move continuously and returns are normally distributed — you use Black-Scholes. The formula has the exact same shape as the binomial methods: "value if exercised" minus "PV of cost to exercise", with each term weighted by a probability.

The two probability-like terms $N(d_1)$ and $N(d_2)$ come from the cumulative normal distribution. $N(d_2)$ is roughly the probability that the option ends up in-the-money. $N(d_1)$ is roughly the option's "delta" — how much the call value changes when the stock moves $\$1$.

Black-Scholes call formula
$$C = N(d_1) \cdot P - N(d_2) \cdot \text{PV}(K)$$
$$d_1 = \frac{\ln(P / \text{PV}(K))}{\sigma\sqrt{t}} + \frac{\sigma\sqrt{t}}{2}$$
$$d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{t}$$
$$\text{PV}(K) = K \cdot e^{-r \cdot t}\;\;\;\text{(continuous compounding!)}$$
Black-Scholes Recipe
1
PV$(K) = K \cdot e^{-rt}$. Use continuous compounding (the $e^x$ button on your calculator), not $1/(1+r)$.
2
$d_1 = \dfrac{\ln(P/\text{PV}(K))}{\sigma\sqrt{t}} + \dfrac{\sigma\sqrt{t}}{2}$
3
$d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{t}$
4
Look up $N(d_1)$ and $N(d_2)$ on the cumulative normal table. If $d$ is negative: $N(-x) = 1 - N(x)$.
5
$C = N(d_1) \cdot P - N(d_2) \cdot \text{PV}(K)$
★ Worked Example S22 Q2a · 10 pts (Microsoft)
Q. $P = \$50$, $K = \$60$, $t = 1$ yr, $r = 1.65\%$, $\sigma = 25\%$. Find call price.
PV(K) = $60 · exp(−0.0165 · 1) = $60 · 0.9836 = $59.02

d_1 = ln(50/59.02) / (0.25 · √1) + (0.25 · √1)/2
    = ln(0.8472) / 0.25 + 0.125
    = −0.1659 / 0.25 + 0.125
    = −0.6634 + 0.125 = −0.54

d_2 = −0.54 − 0.25 = −0.79

N(−0.54) = 1 − N(0.54) = 1 − 0.7054 = 0.2946
N(−0.79) = 1 − N(0.79) = 1 − 0.7854 = 0.2146

C = 0.2946 · $50 − 0.2146 · $59.02
  = $14.73 − $12.67
  = $2.06
Call price $= \$2.06$

TYPE 3D

Put-Call Parity

8/9
What's actually happening here

Imagine two portfolios:

Portfolio A: own a share of stock + own a put with strike $K$. At maturity: you have at least $K$ (you exercise the put if stock falls below $K$) or the stock value (if it's above $K$). Value $= \max(S, K)$.

Portfolio B: own a call with strike $K$ + own a risk-free bond paying $K$. At maturity: bond pays $K$, plus the call gives you any upside above $K$. Value $= \max(S, K)$.

Same payoff in every future scenario → same price today. That's the parity.

Put-Call Parity (no dividends)
$$S + P = C + \text{PV}(K)$$
Stock plus put equals call plus risk-free bond. Use it to find any one of the four prices when you know the other three.
With dividends
$$S + P = C + \text{PV}(K) + \text{PV(div)}$$
A call doesn't entitle you to dividends, but holding the stock does. So the right side gets bumped up by PV of dividends.
★ Worked Example F25 Q2c · 2 pts
Q. $S = \$50$, $K = \$52$, $r_F = 4\%$, $C = \$10.81$, $P = \$10.81$. Verify put-call parity.
LHS: S + P = $50 + $10.81 = $60.81
RHS: C + PV(K) = $10.81 + $52/1.04 = $10.81 + $50.00 = $60.81
$60.81 = $60.81 ✓ Parity holds.
DRILL 3D.1 · $S = \$50$, $K = \$60$, $r_F = 1.65\%$ continuous, $C = \$2.06$. Find $P$.
▸ Show solution
PV$(K) = 60 \cdot \exp(-0.0165) = \$59.02$
$P = C + \text{PV}(K) - S = \$2.06 + \$59.02 - \$50 = $ \$11.08

TYPE 3E

Equity as a Call Option (Distress Cases)

Appears in: S20 · F22 · F23 · F24 (4/9)
What's actually happening here

Here's a deep idea: when a firm has debt, equity holders are like call option holders on the firm's assets. The strike price is the face value of the debt. At maturity:

  • If assets > debt → equity holders pay off the debt, keep the rest (exercise the call).
  • If assets < debt → equity holders walk away, hand the assets to debt holders (let the call expire).

This is why a financially distressed firm's equity has positive value even when assets < debt face value. The call's time value is still positive — assets could rise before maturity.

Symmetrically, debt holders are like sellers of a put. The "option to default" that they "sold" to equity holders reduces the value of the debt below the risk-free amount.

$$\text{Equity} = \text{Call(Assets, } K = \text{FV of Debt})$$
$$\text{Debt} = \text{Assets} - \text{Equity} = \text{PV}(D \,@\, r_F) - \text{Put}$$
Equity is the upside option. Debt is the safe value minus the value of the right to default.
★ Worked Example F24 Q4 (ND Oil)
Q. Assets $=\$50$M now → $\$100$M or $\$25$M in 1 yr. Debt face $=\$60$M (matures in 1 yr). $r_F = 5\%$. 10M shares. Find equity per share.

Treat equity as a call with $K = \$60$M.

Payoffs: Up = max(100 − 60, 0) = $40M; Down = $0
100N + 60M = 40 ; 25N + 60M = 0
N = 40/75 = 0.5333; M = −0.2222

Equity (call) = 0.5333 · $50 + (−0.2222) · ($60/1.05)
    = $26.67 − $12.70 = $13.97M

Per share = $13.97M / 10M shares = $1.40
Share price $\approx \$1.40$. Debt is $\$36.03$M; default-spread implied YTM $\approx 66.5\%$.

Topic 4 · Interest Rate & Currency Swaps

6/9 EXAMS · 15–20 PTS
Pattern Three subparts almost always: draw cash flow diagram → explain back-to-back loan equivalent → value the swap after rates have moved.
Topic 4 Tutorial

What a swap is

A swap is a contract between two parties to exchange streams of cash flows. The two most common types:

  • Interest rate swap: Party A pays a fixed interest rate to Party B; Party B pays a floating rate (like SOFR) to Party A. They're swapping fixed-for-floating on the same notional amount.
  • Currency swap: Party A pays interest in EUR; Party B pays interest in USD. They're swapping currency exposure.

Why bother? Hedging. A bank might lend at fixed rates but borrow at floating rates — a mismatch. A swap can convert one stream into the other and remove the risk.

Two friends with different phone plans. One has unlimited data but no minutes; the other has unlimited minutes but no data. They can keep their plans but agree to "swap" usage — every month, you let your friend use your data, and they let you use their minutes. Both end up with what they actually want, no need to switch carriers.

Critical rule about notionals

The "notional value" is the reference amount that interest payments are calculated against. In an interest rate swap, the notional is never exchanged. Both sides have the same notional in the same currency, so the principals cancel out. Only the interest gets exchanged.

In a currency swap, the notional IS exchanged — both at the start and at the end. That's because the two notionals are in different currencies and can't be assumed equal in value.

The clever trick: synthetic swaps via back-to-back loans

If swap markets didn't exist, you could replicate a swap with two loans: borrow at one rate, lend the same notional at another rate. The principal flows cancel, leaving just the interest rate differential. That's exactly what a swap does.

This is why swap pricing has to be fair — if it weren't, arbitrage between real swaps and synthetic swaps would force prices into line.

Mark-to-market: valuing a swap mid-life

Once interest rates change, the swap is no longer fairly priced — it's now an asset to one party and a liability to the other. To value it:

  • Method 1 (discount): Add a "phantom" principal repayment to each leg. PV both legs at current market rates. The difference is the swap's value.
  • Method 2 (close-out): Imagine entering an offsetting swap at current rates. The cancelable parts drop out. PV the leftover net cash flows. Equivalent to Method 1.

Sign intuition: if you locked in an above-market rate to pay, the swap is a liability (negative value to you).

TYPE 4A

Drawing Swap Cash Flows

7/9
How to set up the notional

If a bank wants to convert a $\$X$ loan paying $r\%$ fixed into floating payments via a swap with fixed rate $s\%$, the swap notional should be $X \cdot r/s$. This way, the dollar amount of fixed interest the bank pays in the swap exactly matches what it receives on the loan — leaving only the floating side as net cash flow.

★ Setup Example F23 Q2 (currency swap)
Party A enters a 3-yr EUR-USD swap. Notional $= €200$M. Spot rate: $€1 = \$1.10$. Fixed EUR rate: $3.5\%$. Fixed USD rate: $4.5\%$. A wants to PAY EUR, RECEIVE USD.

Year 0: A delivers $\$220$M ($=€200$M$\cdot 1.10$), receives $€200$M.

Years 1–3 (interest):

A pays out: €200M · 3.5% = €7.0M/year
A receives: $220M · 4.5% = $9.9M/year

Year 3 (final): A pays back $€200$M, receives back $\$220$M (notional flipped).

Notional flips at start and end; interest exchanged each period.
TYPE 4B

Back-to-Back Loan (Synthetic Swap)

5/9

Standard recipe. If you want to convert fixed-rate income to floating: (1) borrow $\$X$ at fixed rate, (2) lend $\$X$ at floating rate. The principals net to zero. Combined with your existing fixed asset → net effect = receive floating. Same as a swap.

This is also the proof that swaps must price fairly: if the swap rate were too high, you'd arbitrage by entering a synthetic swap at lower cost. Pressure forces equilibrium.

TYPE 5A

Bond Duration

5/9
What's actually happening here

Duration is the weighted average time until you get your money back from a bond. A zero-coupon 10-year bond has duration $= 10$ years (single payment at year 10). A 10-year bond with big coupons has lower duration, because you get cash sooner.

Why finance cares: duration measures interest rate sensitivity. A bond with duration 4 will fall about 4% in price if interest rates rise 1%. A bond with duration 2 will fall only 2%. Duration is essentially a "how dramatic is this bond" score.

$$\text{Duration} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} t \cdot \frac{\text{PV}(CF_t)}{V_0}$$
For each cash flow, multiply (year) × (its PV). Add them up. Divide by total bond price. That's the duration in years.
★ Worked Example F25 Q4b · 4 pts
Q. $\$1{,}000$ face note paying 3 annual $\$400$ payments. YTM $= 12.5\%$. Find duration.
$t$ $CF_t$ PV$(CF_t)$ $t \cdot$ PV$(CF_t)$
1$400$355.56355.56
2$400$316.05632.10
3$400$280.93842.79
$\Sigma$$952.541,830.45
Duration = 1,830.45 / 952.54 = 1.92 years
$D = 1.92$ years
The follow-up question "If rates rise 1%, will this bond fall more or less than a 2-year-duration bond?" → LESS, because $1.92 < 2.0$. Duration $\approx$ % price change for a 1% rate move.
TYPE 5B

FX Forwards (Interest Rate Parity)

5/9
What's actually happening here

If interest rates differ between two countries, the forward exchange rate has to adjust. Otherwise you could borrow in the low-rate country, convert and invest in the high-rate country, and lock in a riskless profit. The forward rate is set such that no such arbitrage exists. That's interest rate parity.

Rule of thumb: the high-interest-rate currency sells at a forward DISCOUNT (expected to depreciate). If USD rates are higher than EUR rates, the forward USD/EUR rate will show fewer EUR per USD in the future — USD weakens.

Generic IRP
$$F(A/B) \cdot (1 + r_B \cdot k/360) = S(A/B) \cdot (1 + r_A \cdot k/360)$$
$$F(A/B) = S(A/B) \cdot \frac{1 + r_A \cdot k/360}{1 + r_B \cdot k/360}$$
"A per B" means how much A you get per 1 unit of B. The currency on top (A) gets its rate on top of the formula.
★ Worked Example F24 Q5c
Q. Spot: $\$1 = $ FC$5$ (so $\$0.20$/FC). 6-mo USD rate $= 4.25\%$ APR; 6-mo FC rate $= 2.75\%$ APR. Find 6-mo forward USD/FC.
F(USD/FC) = 0.20 · (1 + 0.0425/2) / (1 + 0.0275/2)
          = 0.20 · 1.02125 / 1.01375
          = $0.2015 / FC
Forward $= \$0.2015$/FC. FC sells at premium because USD rate $>$ FC rate.
TYPE 5C

Financial & Commodity Futures

5/9
What's actually happening here

The futures price is what the underlying should cost in the future, taking into account the cost of "carrying" it (interest you pay to fund holding it) minus any benefits of holding it (dividends you collect, or convenience yield for commodities).

Foregone cash flows like dividends LOWER the futures price (you'd rather hold the stock and collect dividends; if you buy futures, you skip those, so the futures has to be cheaper to compensate). Storage costs RAISE commodity futures prices (you're spared the cost of warehousing).

Financial futures (stocks etc.)
$$\frac{F}{1 + r_F} = S - \text{PV(foregone CFs)}$$
Commodity futures
$$\frac{F}{1 + r_F} = S + \text{PV(storage)} - \text{PV(convenience)}$$

Q. Stock $=\$50$, 1-yr $r_F = 4\%$, $\$2$ dividend in 90 days, 90-day rate $= 3\%$ APR. Find 1-yr futures price.
PV(div) = $2 / (1 + 0.03/(360/90)) = $2/1.0075 = $1.985

F / 1.04 = $50 − $1.985 = $48.015
F = $48.015 · 1.04 = $49.94
$F = \$49.94$
TYPE 5D

Expectation Theory of Interest Rates

3/9
What's actually happening here

Long-term interest rates are made up of expectations about future short-term rates. If you can earn $r_3 = 4\%$ over 3 years, that should be equivalent to earning $r_1$ for 1 year, then the expected 1-year rate next year, then the expected 1-year rate the year after.

This lets you back out the market's expectation of future rates from observed long-term rates.

$$(1 + r_j)^j = (1 + r_i)^i \cdot (1 + {}_if_j)^{j-i}$$
Long rate compounded equals short rate compounded times the expected forward rate. Use this to back out forward rates.
★ Worked Example S22 Q4e
Q. 3-yr rate $= 4\%$. 1-yr rate $= 2.25\%$. Expected 1-yr rate 1 year out $= 3.85\%$. Find expected 1-yr rate 2 yrs out (${}_2f_3$).
(1.04)³ = (1.0225) · (1.0385) · (1 + ₂f₃)
1.1249 = 1.0625 · (1 + ₂f₃)
1 + ₂f₃ = 1.0587
₂f₃ ≈ 5.87% (textbook says 5.93% with rounding)
TYPE 5E

Payout Decisions: Dividends vs Buybacks

3/9
What's actually happening here

In a Modigliani-Miller world (no taxes, no transaction costs), it doesn't matter whether the firm pays cash to shareholders as dividends or buys back shares. The firm has $\$X$ cash either way. Either pay it as dividend (price drops by div per share) or buy back shares (fewer shares, but same total firm value). Total shareholder wealth: unchanged.

In the real world, deviations from MM make one preferred over the other in different situations.

The Three Critical Dates

  1. Announcement date: firm declares dividend. If dividend was expected, no price reaction.
  2. Ex-dividend date (T+2 settlement → ex-day = record date − 2 trading days; or with T-1 historical convention): price drops by approximately the dividend amount on this day's open. New buyers don't get the dividend.
  3. Payment date: cash actually goes out. No price reaction (everyone already knew).
The buyback question (F25 Q4a) "Buybacks raise stock price by reducing share count and increasing EPS."
FALSE in MM world. Yes, EPS rises mechanically because there are fewer shares. But the firm just paid out cash, so the equity value also fell by exactly that amount. P/E ratio falls to offset. Total wealth per remaining share unchanged.

Real-world deviations from MM

  • Taxes: if dividend tax > capital gains tax, buybacks preferred. If equal, buybacks still preferred for the deferral option.
  • Transaction costs: firms hold cash to avoid future financing fees and act as default insurance.
  • Information signaling: announcing a dividend hike signals confidence — usually positive market reaction.

TYPE 6A

True/False Answer Bank · drilled from past exams

Statements appearing 2+ times across exams

Options · True/False

FALSE "When asset price is high relative to strike, the call price equals the asset price."
In deep ITM, call $\approx S - $ PV$(K)$, not just $S$. The PV of the strike is the "discount" you'll have to pay.
FALSE "Buying a call is the same as selling a put."
A call gives YOU the right to buy. Selling a put gives someone ELSE the right to sell to you. Different obligations, different payoff diagrams.
TRUE "An increase in volatility raises both call and put values."
Both are "long volatility." More uncertainty = more chance of a big payoff = more valuable.

Capital Structure · True/False

FALSE "Because equity is risky, its required return is always higher than the asset return."
If the firm is all-equity (no debt), then $r_E = r_A$. They diverge only when leverage is added.
FALSE "Share buybacks raise the stock price by lowering shares outstanding."
In MM world, no. EPS rises but P/E falls; total wealth unchanged. The price-per-remaining-share is the same as before, because the cash leaving the firm matches the value leaving as bought-back shares.
FALSE "Hostile takeovers are rare outside US/UK due to greater separation of ownership and management abroad."
Backwards. The US/UK have MORE separation (dispersed ownership). Outside, ownership is concentrated (family/state/group), making hostile bids easy to block.
FALSE "USA non-financial firms have negative net equity issues + positive net debt issues, so D/V is naturally rising."
Most investment is funded by retained earnings — which IS equity. So equity grows internally even if no new shares are issued.

Hedging & Derivatives · True/False

FALSE "Adverse selection matters for insurance, moral hazard matters for hedging."
Both adverse selection AND moral hazard apply to insurance. Neither is a major issue for hedging (hedging is just offsetting risk, not assuming asymmetric info).
TRUE "Hedging takes a position that loses value when prices move in your desired direction."
A hedge is the offsetting bet. If your business gains when oil rises, your oil hedge loses. The hedge cancels the bet, intentionally.
FALSE "Storage costs make commodity futures always more expensive than spot."
Convenience yield can offset storage. If convenience > storage + interest, futures < spot ("backwardation"). Common in tight oil markets.

Structure & Governance · True/False

TRUE "A spin-off is a corporate breakup where parent shareholders get shares in the new firm."
Spin-off = pro-rata distribution of new shares to existing SHs. Carve-out = parent sells stake in subsidiary for cash.
FALSE "It's better to lease equipment with rapidly improving technology to avoid value loss."
The lessor knows the equipment will obsolesce and prices it into the lease rate. No free lunch from leasing vs buying tech.

Term Structure · True/False

TRUE "If the term structure is perfectly flat, future expected short-term rates equal current short-term rates."
Definition of flat curve under Expectation Theory. ST = LT now → ST will = ST later.

Foreign Exchange · True/False

FALSE "If country A's interest rate is higher than country B's, then A's currency is expected to appreciate."
Opposite. Interest Rate Parity: the high-rate currency trades at a forward DISCOUNT (expected to depreciate). Otherwise arbitrage.

Short Answer Templates · recurring conceptual questions

SA · Distress Conflict F24 Q5b · F23 · multiple
Q. What behaviors do debtholders fear from shareholders/managers when a firm is in distress? How can DH protect themselves?

The 5 wealth-transfer behaviors:

  • Switch to riskier assets — limited liability lets SH "go for broke."
  • Refuse to fund safe NPV+ projects — gains go entirely to DH.
  • Cash in & run — pay big dividends; transfer wealth out.
  • Issue new senior claims — dilute existing DH priority.
  • Play for time — delay liquidation hoping for good luck.

DH protections: price expected costs into yield (higher $r_D$), or use covenants (financial restrictions that trigger technical default).

SA · Dividend Timing F25 Q4c · F23 Q1b · multiple
Q. When does a stock price react to a dividend?

Three dates:

  • Announcement: No reaction if dividend was expected. Reaction if it surprises.
  • Ex-dividend date (= record date − 1 trading day historically; with T+2 settlement, ex-day = record date − 2): price falls by approximately the dividend amount at the open. New buyers don't get the dividend.
  • Payment date: No reaction.
SA · APV vs WACC F25 Q5f · F22 Q1g · S22 Q1g
Q. Why do APV and WACC give different equity values for the same firm?

The discrepancy comes from how risky the interest tax shield is assumed to be. Under WACC, the firm maintains constant $D/V$, so debt scales with firm value, making the ITS as risky as the assets (discount at $r_U$ → smaller PV). Under APV with permanent debt, the ITS is contractually fixed (only as risky as the debt itself, discount at $r_D$ → larger PV). Same dollars of ITS, two risk assumptions, two values.

SA · Default Spread F24 Q4e
Q. Given a firm's risky debt market value and face value, find the default spread.
YTM = (Face / Market) − 1
Default spread = YTM − r_F

F24 example: Face $\$60$M, Market $\$36.03$M → YTM $= 60/36.03 - 1 = 66.5\%$. With $r_F = 5\%$, spread $=$ $61.5\%$ (deep junk).

Glossary · the terms Bodnar uses without defining

Reference
WACC/wack/
Weighted Average Cost of Capital. Tax-adjusted blended cost of debt + equity. Used to discount unlevered FCF when $D/V$ is constant.
APVAdjusted Present Value
Valuation method where you compute $V_U$ separately, then add PV of tax shields. Used when debt is fixed dollar amount (not constant ratio).
$r_U$ / $r_A$unlevered cost of capital
Required return if the firm had zero debt. A property of the business, not the financing.
$r_E$cost of equity
Required return on equity. Higher than $r_U$ because of leverage.
$r_D$cost of debt
YTM on the firm's debt. Pre-tax cost. Multiplied by $(1 - \tau_C)$ to get after-tax cost.
$\tau_C$corporate tax rate
Greek tau-C. Just notation for "tax rate." If 30%, $\tau_C = 0.30$.
$D/V$, $E/V$capital structure ratios
Debt-to-value, equity-to-value. $V = D + E$ (market values). $D/V + E/V = 1$.
$D/E$debt-to-equity ratio
Used in re-levering formulas. $D/E = (D/V) / (E/V)$. If $D/V = 0.4$, then $D/E = 0.4/0.6 = 0.667$.
FCFFree Cash Flow
Cash from the business after taxes, capex, and working capital changes. The basis for valuation.
FCFEFCF to Equity
FCF after debt service. Used in Flow-to-Equity method. $= $ FCF $- $ after-tax interest $+ $ net debt issuance.
ITSInterest Tax Shield
Tax savings from interest deductibility $= D \cdot r_D \cdot \tau_C$ each period. Why WACC is below $r_U$.
CapExCapital Expenditure
Spending on long-lived assets (PP&E). Reduces FCF.
NWCNet Working Capital
Operating cash tied up: inventory $+$ receivables $-$ payables. Increases reduce FCF.
CAPM/cap-em/
Capital Asset Pricing Model. $r = r_F + \beta \cdot $ MRP. Translates risk (beta) into required return.
$\beta$ (beta)/bay-tah/
Sensitivity to market moves. $\beta = 1$ moves with market; $\beta > 1$ amplifies; $\beta = 0$ is risk-free-like.
MRPMarket Risk Premium
Expected return on the market minus the risk-free rate. The "bonus" for holding equity.
$\sigma$ (sigma)volatility
Standard deviation of returns, annualized. Higher $\sigma$ → options worth more.
$N(d)$normal CDF
Cumulative normal distribution. Look up on table. Probability that a standard normal r.v. $\leq d$.
Strike $(K)$exercise price
Fixed price at which the option holder can buy (call) or sell (put). PV$(K) = K \cdot e^{-r \cdot t}$ for B-S.
YTMYield to Maturity
Internal rate of return on a bond if held to maturity. Solves Price $= \sum CF/(1+\text{YTM})^t$.
Notionalswap face value
Reference amount for swap interest calcs. Not exchanged in interest rate swaps; IS exchanged in currency swaps.
SOFR / LIBORfloating rates
Benchmark short-term rates. SOFR replaced LIBOR in US contracts. Treat as "the floating rate."

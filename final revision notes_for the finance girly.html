Topic No. 01 — The Big One

Cost of Capital, WACC & APV

≈ 20–25 points

If your exam has only one section about money, it's this one. It shows up every single year. Master this and you've already passed.

The Vocab You Need (cheat sheet)

Cost of Capital /the rent on money/
The return investors demand to give you their cash. Think of it as the price tag on borrowing money — except some of it is debt (loans) and some is equity (shareholders).
WACC /wack/ — yes, really
Weighted Average Cost of Capital. The blended price of all your money — debt + equity, weighted by how much of each you use. It's the hurdle rate. Your project must earn more than WACC or it's a money loser.
rU (unlevered cost of capital) /the pure cost/
What capital would cost if you used zero debt. The "all-equity" version. Pretend the company has no loans — what return would shareholders need? That's rU.
Beta (β) /risk score/
How risky a stock is compared to the overall market. Beta = 1 means it moves with the market. Beta = 1.5 means it's 50% more dramatic. Beta = 0.5 means it's chill.
CAPM /cap-em/
The formula that turns a beta into an actual return: r = rF + β × MRP. Translation: "what risk-free pays + bonus for being risky."
D/V and E/V /the proportions/
D = debt, E = equity, V = total firm value (D + E). So D/V = "what % of the company is funded by debt." If D/V = 0.30, it's 30% debt, 70% equity.
Interest Tax Shield (ITS) /the IRS gift/
Interest on debt is tax deductible. So borrowing money literally lowers your tax bill. The savings is the "tax shield." This is why companies love debt.

The Cosmo Memory Trick: Think of cost of capital like dating. Equity holders are high-maintenance girlfriends — they want a high return because they take all the risk. Debt holders are easy — fixed monthly payments and they leave. WACC = your average dating cost across both. APV = "what if I were single (no debt)... plus a bonus for the tax break of being in a relationship."

The 4-Step Formula Routine

Find rU (unlevered cost of capital). They give you data on a similar industry. Use: βU = (D/V)·βD + (E/V)·βE. Then rU = rF + βU × MRP.
Value the project unlevered. VU = FCF₁ ÷ (rU − g). If no growth, just FCF/rU. NPV = VU − initial cost.
WACC method (when D/V is constant): rWACC = rU − (D/V)·rD·τC. Then VL = FCF₁ ÷ (rWACC − g). Equity = VL × (E/V).
APV method (when debt is fixed forever): VL = VU + D × τC. That's literally it. Equity = VL − D.
rWACC = rU − (D/V) · rD · τC

What's actually happening: WACC takes your "pure" cost of capital and gives you a discount because the government subsidizes your debt (interest is tax-deductible). The bigger your debt and the higher your tax rate, the bigger the discount. That's why debt — in moderation — makes companies more valuable.

The cost of capital is the toll your project pays to use other people's money.
Topic No. 02 — The Math-Heavy One

Options & Black-Scholes

≈ 15–20 points

Options are like reservations. You pay a small fee today for the right to do something later — but you don't have to.

The Vocab You Need

Call Option /the right to buy/
The right (not obligation) to buy something at a fixed price (the strike) by a future date. You buy a call when you think the price will go UP.
Put Option /the right to sell/
The right (not obligation) to sell something at a fixed price by a future date. You buy a put when you think the price will go DOWN — or for insurance.
Strike Price (K) /the fixed price/
The agreed-upon price written into the option contract. Doesn't change.
Volatility (σ) /sigma — the drama level/
How wildly the price swings around. Higher volatility = more drama = options are worth MORE (because more chance of a big payoff). Both calls and puts get more expensive.
In/Out of the Money /profitable or not/
A call is "in the money" if stock price > strike (you'd exercise). "Out of the money" if stock < strike (you'd let it expire worthless).

The Cosmo Way to Remember Calls vs Puts: A CALL is like calling dibs on a designer bag at today's price — you'll buy it later if it goes up in value. A PUT is like getting a return receipt — you can put it back at the locked-in price if things go south. You always want low and dump high. Calls let you buy low. Puts let you sell high.

Black-Scholes (the famous formula)

C = N(d₁) · P − N(d₂) · PV(K)

This looks scary. It's not. Read it like this: "Expected stock value if you exercise" minus "expected cost of exercising." What's left over is the option's value today.

N(d₁) = the option's "delta" — how sensitive the call is to stock moves.
N(d₂) = the probability the option ends up profitable.
N( ) just means "look this number up on the normal distribution table." It's a percentile.

The Step-by-Step Recipe

PV(K) = K × e^(−r·t). Yes, you must use continuous compounding here, not (1+r). The e is on your calculator.
d₁ = ln(P / PV(K)) ÷ (σ√t) + (σ√t)/2
d₂ = d₁ − σ√t (just subtract σ√t from d₁)
Look up N(d₁) and N(d₂) on the normal table. If d is negative, use N(−x) = 1 − N(x).
Plug in: C = N(d₁)·P − N(d₂)·PV(K)

Put-Call Parity (memorize this — free points!)

S + P = C + PV(K)

Stock + Put = Call + safe bond. If you know any 3, you can solve for the 4th. They love asking this in disguise — like "given the call price, find the put price." Just rearrange.

Volatility intuition (always asked): If your friend thinks volatility will be HIGHER than you assumed, she wants to BUY your call from you — because in her eyes, you're underpricing it. Higher vol = options worth more. Always.

The Two Binomial Methods (one-period)

Replicating Portfolio

Build a fake option using stock + bond.

Solve: N·Pu + M·K = Payoff_up
And: N·Pd + M·K = Payoff_down

Then: Call = N·P₀ + M·PV(K)

Risk-Neutral Probability

Pretend everyone is neutral about risk.

Solve for ProbRN(up) so that:
ProbRN·R_up + (1−ProbRN)·R_down = rF

Then: Call = [ProbRN·Payoff_up + (1−ProbRN)·Payoff_down] / (1+rF)

Both methods give the exact same answer. Pick whichever feels easier.

Topic No. 03 — The Calculator One

Free Cash Flow & Project Eval

≈ 15–25 points

"Free cash flow" is the actual cash the project hands you at the end of a year — after taxes, after expenses, after reinvestment. It's the only number that matters for valuation.

The Vocab You Need

Free Cash Flow (FCF) /the take-home/
The cash the business produces that's actually free — meaning you could pay it out to investors without hurting operations. This is what you discount to value the company.
Depreciation /dep-RE-shee-AY-shun/
An accounting fiction. You bought equipment 5 years ago — accountants pretend it loses value each year so you can write it off as an "expense." But no actual cash leaves your bank account each year. It's a tax trick, nothing more.
CapEx /cap-ex/
Capital Expenditures. Money spent buying long-term stuff: factories, machinery, computers. Big upfront purchases that the business will use for years.
NWC /Net Working Capital/
Cash tied up in day-to-day operations — inventory, money owed to you by customers, etc. When the business grows, NWC grows (more inventory needed). That's cash locked up in the business, not available to investors.
τC (tau-C) /the tax rate/
The corporate tax rate. Just a Greek letter for "tax." If the rate is 30%, τC = 0.30.
FCF = (Revenue − costs − depreciation) · (1 − τC) + depreciation − ΔNWC − CapEx + after-tax salvage

Why subtract depreciation then add it back? Because of the tax trick. You subtract it first to lower your taxable income (paying less tax). Then you add it back because you didn't actually pay it — it's not real cash leaving the company. The end result: you save tax money on a "fake" expense. Genius? Annoying? Both.

Picture FCF like your monthly paycheck after EVERYTHING:
Revenue = your salary
Costs = rent + groceries + Pilates
Tax = the IRS's cut
CapEx = that new laptop you had to buy
ΔNWC = restocking your skincare
What's left at the end of the month? That's FCF. That's what you can actually save or send home.

Time Zero is Special

At time 0, the business hasn't done anything yet. So FCF₀ = − initial CapEx − initial NWC. It's just the upfront investment, all negative.

After-Tax Salvage (when assets get sold at the end)

After-tax salvage = Salvage − (Salvage − Book Value) · τC

If you sell an old machine for more than its book value, you owe tax on the difference.

Topic No. 04 — Free Points

True / False & Short Answer

≈ 15–20 points

These come up every year. Memorize the answers below — these are the actual ones professor Bodnar likes to recycle.

FALSE"Share buybacks raise the stock price by lowering shares outstanding."
In an MM (Modigliani-Miller) world, no — the pie just gets sliced into fewer pieces. EPS goes up but P/E ratio falls to offset. Total value of equity = unchanged. Buybacks just return cash to shareholders.
TRUE"If the yield curve is flat, future expected short rates equal current short rates."
Definition of a flat curve. No surprises in the future.
TRUE"A spin-off is when shareholders of the parent get shares in the new firm."
That's literally the definition. (A carve-out is different — that's a partial IPO of a subsidiary.)
FALSE"Hostile takeovers are rare outside the US/UK because of greater separation of ownership and management abroad."
Backwards. Hostile takeovers are MORE common where ownership is dispersed (US/UK). Outside, ownership is more concentrated (family-owned, etc.) so hostile takeovers are blocked easily.
FALSE"Adverse selection is for insurance, moral hazard is for hedging."
Both apply to BOTH. Adverse selection = bad people opt-in. Moral hazard = people behave riskier once protected.
FALSE"Hedging is taking a position that gains when prices move in your favor."
Opposite. Hedging means you take a position that LOSES when prices move in your favor — because you already win on the underlying business. It cancels out the risk. Boring, on purpose.
TRUE"Equity required return is always higher than asset return."
Equity is leveraged — debt holders get paid first. Equity holders take the leftover risk, so they demand more.
FALSE"Storage costs make commodity futures always more expensive than spot."
Not always — convenience yield (the benefit of physically owning the commodity) can offset storage costs. Sometimes futures < spot (called "backwardation").
FALSE"It's better to lease tech equipment because it gets outdated quickly."
The market already prices that in. Whoever buys the equipment knows it depreciates fast and charges accordingly. There's no free lunch.

Dividend Timing (always asked)

When does the stock price drop? On the ex-dividend date — not the announcement date, not the payment date. The price drops by approximately the dividend amount, because new buyers won't get the dividend. Announcement date = no reaction (already expected). Payment date = no reaction (cash leaves but everyone already knew).

Topic No. 05 — Likely

Bonds & Duration

≈ 5–10 points

Duration sounds technical but it's just a weighted average of when you get your money.

Duration /the gravity-of-cash-flow/
The average time it takes for you to get paid back, weighted by how much each payment is worth today. A 10-year bond with no coupons has duration = 10. A 10-year bond with big coupons has lower duration because you get cash earlier.
Duration = Σ [t · PV(CFt)] ÷ V₀

For each cash flow, multiply year × PV of that cash flow. Add them all up. Divide by the bond's total value today. That's the duration in years.

The interest-rate sensitivity rule: Higher duration = bigger price swings when rates change. If rates rise 1%, a bond with duration 4 falls roughly 4%. A bond with duration 2 falls roughly 2%. Duration is basically a "how dramatic is this bond" score.

Topic No. 06 — Possibly

Futures & Forwards

≈ 5–10 points

A futures contract locks in a price today for a transaction that happens later. They show up in 1 question — usually a True/False or a "find the futures price" calc.

The Three Formulas to Memorize

Financial Futures (stocks, etc.)

Futures Price ÷ (1 + rF) = Spot − PV(foregone cash flows)

Foregone cash flows = dividends you miss out on by buying futures instead of holding the stock.

Commodity Futures (oil, gold, etc.)

Futures Price ÷ (1 + rF) = Spot + PV(storage) − PV(convenience)

Storage = it costs money to keep the oil somewhere. Convenience yield = the benefit of physically having it (rare events, supply chains).

FX Forwards (currency)

Forward(A/B) · (1 + rB) = Spot(A/B) · (1 + rA)

"A per B" — the currency you're getting goes on top.

Big-picture intuition: The futures price ≈ what the spot price should be in the future, adjusted for the cost of carrying the asset (interest, storage) minus benefits of holding it (dividends, convenience).

You don't need to be a finance girlie. You just need to recognize the question and remember the formula.
